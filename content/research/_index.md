      title: General Overview
      body: |
        My research examines how marine biodiversity hotspots in the Cenozoic have changed through time, specifically focusing on how fish entered and diversified in the Indo–West Pacific (IWP). Current hypothesis is that biodiversity hotspots are not fixed, and have shifted geographically (“hotspot hopping”), from the Tethys region, proto-mediterranean, to the modern IWP in response to tectonic and environmental change.
        
        Using underutilized fossil assemblages, this work reconstructs more accurate patterns of species richness (“hotspot hopping”) and lineage distributions (“lineage sliding”).

  - block: markdown
    content:
      title: "Block 1: Tropical Fossil Fish Faunas"
      body: |
        A major limitation in reconstructing the rise of the IAA hotspot is the lack of any broad overview of Cenozoic tropical fossil fish faunas globally. Our current results show that the establishment of essentially modern IWP nearshore assemblages happened by the early Neogene; much earlier than suggested by molecular studies.

  - block: markdown
    content:
      title: "Block 2: First Fossil Spadefish"
      body: |
        We report the first fossil spadefish from the Indo-Pacific, discovered in the middle Eocene (ca. 47 Ma) Habib Rahi Formation of Pakistan. Preserved as an impression, this individual intact skeleton closely resembles †Eoplatax from Bolca.

  - block: markdown
    content:
      title: "Block 3: Otolith Data & Rarefaction"
      body: |
        I present data from 80 localities representing nearly 100,000 individual otolith fossils. Using coverage-based rarefaction, we've found a decline in biodiversity in the West Tethys during the late Miocene, challenging previous literature on marine invertebrate migration patterns.
